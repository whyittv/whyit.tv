The Pepsi Story

Origins: Brad's Drink

Pepsi started the same way Coca-Cola did—as a patent medicine. In 1893, pharmacist Caleb Bradham created "Brad's Drink" at his drugstore in New Bern, North Carolina. He renamed it Pepsi-Cola in 1898, claiming it aided digestion (pepsin) and provided energy (cola nuts).

Unlike Coke's steady rise, Pepsi's early history was marked by struggle. The company went bankrupt twice—in 1923 and 1931—casualties of sugar price volatility and mismanagement. It survived through reinvention, emerging each time with new ownership and new strategies.

The Nickel Strategy

Pepsi's first breakthrough came from desperation. In the Depression era, the company offered 12 ounces for five cents—twice the amount of Coke for the same price. It wasn't positioning. It was survival. But it worked, and it established a pattern: Pepsi would always be the value alternative, the brand willing to give you more.

The famous radio jingle "Pepsi-Cola hits the spot, twelve full ounces, that's a lot" became one of the first advertising earworms in American history.

The Pepsi Generation

The pivotal shift came in 1963. Instead of competing on price, Pepsi decided to compete on identity. The "Pepsi Generation" campaign didn't talk about taste or value—it talked about who you were. Drinking Pepsi meant being young, energetic, and forward-looking. Drinking Coke meant being your parents.

This wasn't just advertising. It was the invention of lifestyle marketing. Pepsi understood that in a world of product parity, the real product was identity itself.

The Pepsi Challenge

In 1975, Pepsi escalated the Cola Wars with blind taste tests showing consumers preferred Pepsi. The campaign was brilliant provocation—it forced Coke to respond, eventually triggering the "New Coke" debacle of 1985. Pepsi had managed to make the market leader doubt itself.

But there's an irony: despite winning taste tests, Pepsi has never overtaken Coke in market share. Blind preference doesn't translate to branded preference. The Pepsi Challenge proved the power of branding even as it challenged it.

Beyond Soda

Perhaps Pepsi's smartest move was recognizing that soda wasn't the future. The 1965 merger with Frito-Lay created PepsiCo, a snack and beverage conglomerate. Today, Frito-Lay generates roughly two-thirds of PepsiCo's operating profit. Pepsi the brand is almost secondary to Pepsi the company.

The Missteps

Pepsi's willingness to take risks has also produced spectacular failures. The 2017 Kendall Jenner ad—depicting the model handing a Pepsi to a police officer at a protest—was pulled within 24 hours after intense backlash for trivializing social movements. It became a case study in how brands can misread cultural moments.

Open Questions

Can a challenger brand maintain its identity when it becomes an establishment player? As soda consumption declines, what is the role of the Pepsi brand itself within PepsiCo? And in an era skeptical of corporate activism, can Pepsi find a new way to connect with youth culture without overreaching?

The perpetual challenger faces new challenges—and the answers aren't in a blind taste test.
