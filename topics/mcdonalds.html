McDonald's

The golden arches didn't build themselves.

The Story

How a milkshake machine salesman turned two brothers' burger stand into the most recognized restaurant on Earth—and the system that made it possible.

In 1954, Ray Kroc walked into a small hamburger stand in San Bernardino, California, run by brothers Dick and Mac McDonald. What he saw wasn't just a restaurant—it was a system. The "Speedee Service System" had reimagined fast food as an assembly line, delivering consistent quality at unprecedented speed.

Kroc didn't invent the hamburger or the franchise model. What he built was something more significant: a real estate empire disguised as a restaurant chain. McDonald's Corporation owns or leases the land and buildings, then charges franchisees rent that scales with their success. The company makes money whether or not you order fries.

Today, McDonald's serves 69 million customers daily across 40,000 locations in over 100 countries. The golden arches are more widely recognized than the Christian cross. But the same systems that enabled this scale—standardization, efficiency, franchise economics—create ongoing tensions around wages, health, labor practices, and local food cultures.

The McDonald brothers got bought out in 1961 for $2.7 million. The handshake deal for their promised 1% royalty was never honored. Ray Kroc built an empire. The question isn't whether McDonald's succeeded—it's what that success required and who bears its costs.

Key Facts

  • Founded: 1940 (original), 1955 (Kroc's franchise corporation)
  • Founders: Richard and Maurice McDonald; Ray Kroc (corporation)
  • Headquarters: Chicago, Illinois
  • Daily customers: ~69 million worldwide
  • Locations: 40,000+ in 100+ countries
  • Revenue model: Real estate ownership + franchise fees + rent
  • 2023 Revenue: ~$23 billion
  • Employees: ~150,000 corporate; ~2 million system-wide
